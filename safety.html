Safety Guidelines & Licensing

Fly responsibly, legally, and with confidence

Essential Drone Do's and Don'ts

✓ DO's

  • Always check weather conditions before flying
  • Keep your drone within visual line of sight
  • Fly below 400 feet in uncontrolled airspace
  • Respect privacy and property rights
  • Register your drone with the FAA (if over 0.55 lbs)
  • Check for temporary flight restrictions (TFRs)
  • Conduct pre-flight inspections of your equipment
  • Use apps like B4UFLY to check airspace status
  • Fly during daylight or civil twilight with proper lighting
  • Maintain a safe distance from people and airports

✗ DON'Ts

  • Never fly near airports without authorization
  • Don't fly over people or moving vehicles
  • Never fly under the influence of drugs or alcohol
  • Don't fly in National Parks (it's prohibited)
  • Never interfere with emergency operations
  • Don't fly over stadiums or sporting events
  • Never fly at night without proper waiver and lighting
  • Don't ignore airspace restrictions
  • Never fly recklessly or dangerously
  • Don't assume you can fly anywhere you want

⚠️ Important Reminder

California has additional state laws regarding drones. Always research local ordinances in the county or city where you plan to fly. Some municipalities have specific restrictions on drone use in public parks and spaces.

Getting Your Part 107 License

If you're planning to fly commercially or want to become a certified drone pilot, you'll need a Part 107 Remote Pilot Certificate from the FAA. Even for recreational pilots, understanding Part 107 regulations is valuable.

How to Get Your Part 107 License

Follow these steps to become a certified remote pilot:

1

Eligibility

Be at least 16 years old, able to read and speak English, and physically and mentally fit to fly.

2

Study

Study the aeronautical knowledge areas. Free resources available online or use paid courses.

3

Register

Create an FAA Tracking Number (FTN) at iacra.faa.gov to schedule your test.

4

Take Test

Pass the Part 107 exam at an FAA-approved testing center. Cost is $175.

5

Get Certified

Complete your application in IACRA and receive your certificate in 6-8 weeks.

Recreational vs Commercial Flying

Recreational Flying: If you're flying purely for fun, you must follow The Exception for Recreational Flyers. This means flying for enjoyment only, following community-based safety guidelines, keeping your drone within visual line of sight, and notifying airports and air traffic control when flying within 5 miles of an airport.

Commercial Flying: Any flight that's not purely recreational requires a Part 107 license. This includes real estate photography, paid videography, inspections, or any flight where you're compensated. Even posting drone footage on YouTube with monetization enabled technically requires Part 107 certification.
